ABSTRACT 

This paper aims to examine the potential impact of artificial intelligence in judicial systems 
and assess the readiness of legal institutions and professionals to adopt and implement AI-
based  tools  in  adjudication  and  court  management.  Artificial  intelligence  (AI)  is 
increasingly being integrated into various areas of judicial systems. From legal analysis to 
decision  support  tools,  from  court  administration  to  automated  adjudication,  AI 
technologies have the potential to improve the speed, accuracy, and efficiency of judicial 
processes.  This  article  outlines  the  general  framework  of  AI  implementation  in  the 
judiciary,  explores  the  concept  of  “AI  judges,”  and  examines  the  applicability  of  such 
technologies  in  the  Republic  of  North  Macedonia.  Considering  the  current  legal  and 
constitutional context, as well as technological limitations, the integration of AI into North 
Macedonia’s judiciary appears conditionally feasible. This study has been conducted based 
on a diverse range of sources, including various books, scholarly articles, peer-reviewed 
journals, and official reports published by key public institutions. Accordingly, the article 
examines the use of artificial intelligence in the judiciary and legal system under four main 
subheadings:  theoretical  framework,  ethical  concerns,  technological  infrastructure,  and 
legal  regulations.  Each  of  these  dimensions  has  been  explored  in  a  more  concrete  and 
detailed manner. As a result, it is argued that for North Macedonia to implement AI systems 
within its judiciary, it should first develop a comprehensive national AI strategy, initiate 
both legislative and constitutional reforms with reference to comparative law, and align 
with EU standards. Furthermore, it should adhere to the fundamental principles outlined 
throughout this study. However, critical questions remain regarding how such applications 
can be reconciled with fundamental legal principles such as the rule of law, the right to a 
fair trial, transparency, and accountability. This study contributes to the emerging literature 
on AI in justice systems by offering a context-specific analysis of its applicability within a 
developing legal and institutional framework, using North Macedonia as a case study. 

Keywords: Artificial Intelligence, AI Judges, North Macedonia, Legal Framework, Ethics 
and Digital Justice 

 
 
1.  Introduction 

The  use  of  artificial  intelligence  (AI)  technologies  in  the  legal  field  particularly  within  judicial 
systems  has  been  drawing  increasing  attention  worldwide  due  to  its  potential  to  enhance  the 
efficiency  and  effectiveness  of  justice  delivery.  In  parallel,  the  concept  of  the  “AI  judge”  has 
emerged, primarily aimed at automating decision-making processes in simple legal disputes. For 
instance, in Estonia, a pilot project has been proposed involving a “robot judge” system designed 
to resolve small claims cases. Under this model, parties would submit their documents digitally, 
after which the AI system would issue a decision, subject to appeal before a human judge. Similarly, 
since 2017, China's Internet Courts have handled millions of cases online with the assistance of AI 
judges.  In  many  proceedings,  virtual  judges  in  the  form  of  holograms  have  conducted  video 
interactions  with  parties,  received  evidence,  and  rendered  decisions.  While  these  developments 
demonstrate that the integration of AI into judicial processes is practically feasible, they also give 
rise to significant ethical, technical, and legal debates. 

This study aims to examine the implementation of artificial intelligence in the judiciary within the 
context of North Macedonia. As the country undergoes digital transformation and legal reforms in 
line with its EU accession process, the applicability of AI-based systems will be addressed across 
three  main  dimensions.  First,  ethical  concerns  will  be  explored,  including  impartiality, 
transparency, human rights, and the accountability of decisions. Second, the focus will shift to the 
technological infrastructure - namely the current level of digitalization, data management capacity, 
and  the  IT  systems  required  for  AI  integration.  Finally,  legal  regulations  will  be  analyzed, 
particularly  the  existing  legal  framework  of  North  Macedonia,  its  alignment  with  EU  and 
international  standards,  and  potential  regulatory  gaps.  Within  this  framework,  international 
experiences  and  examples from  EU practices  will  be used to  assess  North Macedonia’s  current 
position and future prospects. 

2.  Theoretical Framework  

Artificial intelligence (AI) is defined as a set of algorithms designed to equip machines with human-
like cognitive abilities such as learning, reasoning, and decision-making. (Susskind, 2019). The 
development of AI technologies has gained strength through concrete applications in various fields 
such  as  education,  law,  healthcare,  finance,  and  security,  enabling  the  automation  of  numerous 
processes that traditionally required human intervention. (Selimi, Saracevic, & Useini, 2020). The 
rise  of  AI  in  the  legal field  has  accelerated  with  the  effective  application  of  big  data  analytics, 
natural language processing (NLP), and machine learning techniques to legal texts. (Surden, 2014). 

Globally, the literature on AI in the judiciary presents a dual narrative: one of innovation and one 
of caution. On the one hand, AI offers tools for legal research, case management, risk assessment, 
and preliminary adjudication. (Zouridis, van Eck, M. L.,, & Bovens, 2019). For instance, China’s 
Internet  Courts  have  handled  over  three  million  disputes  using  digital  platforms  and  AI-driven 
tools. (Vasdani, 2020). Estonia has proposed an AI adjudicator for claims under €7,000, with the 
intention of improving efficiency in small claims cases. (Niiler, 2019). On the other hand, academic 
scholars  raise  concerns  about  algorithmic  bias,  transparency,  and  human  rights  implications. 
(McGregor,  2021).  The  Council  of  Europe’s  (2018)  Ethical  Charter  on  AI  in  the  judiciary 
underscores the need for human oversight, transparency, and respect for fundamental rights. 

This initiative is particularly noteworthy as it would represent one of the first instances of granting 
decision-making authority - albeit limited - to an AI system. Similarly, in some U.S. states, risk 
assessment algorithms (e.g., COMPAS) are used to assist judges in criminal proceedings; however, 
such tools have been criticized for allegedly exhibiting racial bias. (Estonia, 2019). In the United 
Kingdom, an AI-powered chatbot called DoNotPay has assisted citizens in drafting appeals against 
parking  fines,  leading  to  the  cancellation  of  thousands  of  erroneous  tickets.  (Angwin,  Larson, 
Mattu, & Kirchner, 2016). 

Academic  studies  examine  the  role  of  AI  in  judicial  processes  from  both  opportunity  and  risk 
perspectives.  AI  holds  significant  potential to enhance  efficiency  by  supporting human labor in 
areas  such  as  legal  research,  precedent  analysis,  and  the  classification  of  petitions  and  legal 
documents (Ali, 2024). For instance, in complex case files, AI algorithms can rapidly scan relevant 
case law and legislation, providing judges with comprehensive information thereby contributing to 
more informed and consistent decision-making. (Dönmez, 2025). Indeed, a recent study suggests 
that the chronic workload and case backlog issues within the North Macedonian judiciary could be 
alleviated  through  AI-assisted  automation.  (Korbayram  &  Angeleska,  2023).  In  particular,  the 
automation of routine and straightforward procedural tasks by AI could allow judges and court staff 
to  concentrate  on  more  complex  and  substantive  matters.  However,  the  literature  consistently 
underscores  ethical  concerns.  It  emphasizes  the  need  for  transparency  in  decision-making 
processes,  the  elimination  of  algorithmic  bias,  the  establishment  of  oversight  mechanisms  for 
errors, and ultimately, the responsibility of a human judge to oversee and validate AI-generated 
outcomes. (Korbayram & Angeleska, 2023). The European Ethical Charter on the Use of Artificial 
Intelligence in Judicial Systems, adopted by the Council of Europe in 2018 (Council of Europe, 
2018), It recommends that AI applications comply with the principles of respect for human rights, 
non-discrimination, quality and security, transparency and impartiality, and user control (human 
oversight (Nuredin, 2022b).  

These principles advocate for a cautious approach toward granting AI systems autonomous and 
unchecked judicial authority. (Council of Europe, 2018). Consequently, the prevailing view in the 
literature is that AI should not replace human judges entirely, but rather be implemented gradually 
and carefully as a supportive tool within the judicial process. As Estonian President Kersti Kaljulaid 
has noted, when AI takes over routine and labor-intensive tasks, human judges can focus on aspects 
such as empathy and moral judgment - areas that machines can never truly replicate; provided, of 
course, that AI systems are deployed in a secure and transparent manner. (Estonia, 2019). 

3.  Ethical Dimensions 

The use of artificial intelligence in the judiciary raises significant ethical questions. The first major 
concern is how to uphold the principle of impartiality. AI algorithms carry the risk of reflecting the 
biases  present  in  the  datasets  on  which  they  are  trained.  For  instance,  in  the  United  States,  the 
COMPAS risk assessment AI system used in criminal courts was found to assign higher risk scores 
to Black defendants, thereby contributing to discriminatory outcomes. (Estonia, 2019). Such cases 
demonstrate that AI decisions can unintentionally be biased based on factors like race, gender, or 
social status. The prohibition of discrimination is explicitly emphasized as one of the core ethical 
principles  of  the  Council  of  Europe  regarding  AI.  Accordingly,  AI  systems  must  ensure  equal 
treatment  for  all  individuals  and  must  not  adversely  affect  disadvantaged  or  vulnerable  groups. 
(Council of Europe, 2018). 

The second critical issue concerns the principles of transparency and explainability. AI judges or 
decision-support systems must not operate as “black boxes” in other words, the logic behind their 
conclusions must be comprehensible and explainable. One of the fundamental principles of law is 
the right to a reasoned judgment, which also applies to AI-generated decisions; the rationale behind 
the  outcome  must  be  meaningful  and  accessible.  However,  complex  algorithms  such  as  deep 
learning often produce models that are difficult for humans to interpret. This lack of transparency 
poses challenges for the public acceptance and accountability of judicial decisions. 

Accountability  becomes  especially  crucial  in  this  context,  raising  the  question:  who  bears 
responsibility when an AI-assisted decision is flawed or unjust? Is it the system developers, the 
judicial  authorities,  or  the  human  judge  who  ultimately  approves  the  decision?  This  ambiguity 
represents a critical gap that must be addressed before AI can be responsibly used in the judiciary. 
Furthermore, there must be clear mechanisms for correcting errors and appealing decisions made 
with AI involvement. Indeed, in China’s AI judge pilot programs, decisions made by AI systems 
are  subject  to  review  by  human  judges  in  higher  courts  an  example  of  how  the  principle  of 
accountability can be operationalized in practice. (Vasdani, 2020).  

In countries like North Macedonia, which are parties to the European Convention on Human Rights 
(ECHR), no practice should be allowed that undermines the fairness of judicial proceedings, as 
guaranteed under Article 6 of the Convention, the right to a fair trial. In this context, the use of AI 
must  not  conflict  with  fundamental  rights  such  as  access  to  a  court,  equality  of  arms,  or  the 
presumption  of  innocence.  For  instance,  a  fully  automated  decision-making  system  could 
potentially limit the defendant’s right to appear before a judge or to have a face-to-face hearing 
with the decision-maker. Therefore, before such systems are implemented, their legal and ethical 
legitimacy must be carefully examined. (Korbajram & Hoca, 2021). 

The human factor and human dignity also occupy a central place in the ethical dimension  (Nuredin 
& Nuredin, 2023) of AI in the judiciary. Judicial proceedings are not merely about mechanically 
applying legal rules (Nuredin, 2016) - they require human judgment and moral reasoning. A human 
judge, by listening to the testimonies of victims and defendants in person, can perceive nuances in 
speech,  emotional  reactions,  and  signs  of  remorse  -  elements  that  carry  significant  weight  in 
delivering justice. In contrast, current AI systems lack emotional intelligence and the capacity for 
ethical judgment. As a result, AI judges are often criticized for their lack of a “human touch,” which 
may undermine the inherently humane character of the judicial process. (Council of Europe, 2018). 
Public trust in the judiciary may also be affected at this stage; the perception that justice is being 
administered by machines, especially in the absence of sufficient transparency can make it more 
difficult  for  citizens  to  accept  judicial  decisions.  To  build  and  maintain  trust,  it  must  be  made 
explicitly clear that in all cases where AI is used as a tool, the final responsibility for the decision 
lies with a human judge, and that AI serves only as an assistive mechanism. 

This reflects the core of the “user control” principle outlined in the Council of Europe’s Ethical 
Charter, which asserts that while AI can support judicial processes, judicial discretion must always 
remain  in  human  hands.  In  conclusion,  from  an  ethical  standpoint,  it  is  essential  that  North 
Macedonia  establish  rules  and  oversight  mechanisms  that  safeguard  the  principles  of  non-
discrimination, transparency, respect for human rights, and accountability before implementing AI 
systems in the judiciary. (Council of Europe, 2018). 

4.  Analysis of Technological Infrastructure 

The integration of AI into the judicial system requires a robust technological infrastructure. As a 
first step, case files, court decisions, and legal documents must be digitized and made accessible 
through integrated databases. In North Macedonia’s judiciary, an Automated Case Management 
Information System (ACCMIS) is currently in use. However, the infrastructure of this system is 
significantly outdated it operates on a Windows Server 2008 platform, with each court managing 
its  own  separate  database,  and  the  capacity  of  the  central  server  remains  limited.  (Janevski  & 
Zoroska Kamilovska, 2009).  

Although the Information Center within the Supreme Court provides certain centralized services 
through  data  replication  from  all  courts,  it  is  evident  that  the  current  data  storage  and  sharing 
capacities  are  insufficient,  and  the  existing  systems  are  in  need  of  a  comprehensive  upgrade. 
Moreover, auxiliary digital systems such as the court audio recording system (FEMIDA) also suffer 
from capacity limitations. Given the demands of AI applications - particularly in terms of big data 
management  and  real-time  processing  -  these  infrastructural  shortcomings  pose  a  significant 
obstacle to effective implementation. (Gjorgjioska, Gorgieva D., & Stoileva., 2020). 

In  recent  years,  the  government  of  North  Macedonia  has  taken  strategic  steps  to  accelerate 
digitalization in the judiciary. The Ministry of Justice’s 2020-2022 Strategic Plan and the 2019-
2024 Information and Communication Technology Strategy have identified the digitalization of 
judicial processes and the establishment of a modern e-justice system as key priorities. Within the 
scope of these plans, concrete objectives have been set, including the development of a centralized 
e-Court portal, the modernization of case management systems for courts and prosecutors’ offices, 
the  standardization  of  data,  and  training  programs  to  help  judicial  personnel  adapt  to  new 
technologies. (Ministry of Justice, 2020).  

Indeed, in November 2024, the Judiciary Working Group of the EU National Convention in North 
Macedonia  issued  recommendations  to  the  government,  emphasizing  the  completion  of  an 
interoperability platform for inter-court systems and the full implementation of the e-Court system. 
(National Convention on the EU in the RN Macedonia, Acc. 2025). Through these initiatives, it is 
envisioned that key procedures such as filing lawsuits, submitting petitions and documents, and 
monitoring  hearings  can  be  conducted  entirely  in  digital  form,  enabling  the  end-to-end 
digitalization of judicial processes. The same report also recommended the establishment of public 
oversight mechanisms via digital platforms-allowing civil society to monitor the progress of court 
cases—as well as cybersecurity and data protection training for members of the judiciary, and the 
adaptation of successful digital justice models from EU member states. These recommendations 
indicate that North Macedonia is actively working to address its technological infrastructure gaps 
and  lay  the  groundwork  for  the  future  integration  of  advanced  technologies  such  as  artificial 
intelligence. (National Convention on the EU in the RN Macedonia, Acc. 2025). 

When discussing infrastructure for AI applications, it is important to consider not only software but 
also hardware and human resources. Implementing such systems will require servers capable of 
processing large volumes of judicial data, cloud computing services, or high-capacity data centers. 
In North Macedonia, the country’s limited technical capacity has also slowed the progress of the 
working group established in 2021 to develop a National AI Strategy (Ali & Skender, 2020). Key 
challenges identified in both the preparation and implementation of this strategy include the lack 
of  sufficient  data,  a  shortage  of  qualified  experts, and  inadequate technical  infrastructure. (Law 
Gratis Reports., 2025). 

Experts  have  noted  that  developing  a  comprehensive  AI  strategy  is  further  complicated  by  the 
ongoing brain drain, emphasizing the need to retain well-trained young IT professionals within the 
country. To address these shortcomings, it has been pledged that an AI strategy aligned with EU 
standards will be developed with the support of international organizations such as the World Bank 
and UNDP. In addition, private sector initiatives in the ICT field are being encouraged. The Fund 
for Innovation and Technology Development (FITD) provides grants to start-up projects in the AI 
sector and has supported the creation of applications such as “ADA,” the first Macedonian AI-
powered digital assistant. These types of digital assistants, which offer basic advisory services to 
citizens within public administration, help build institutional experience and foster broader public 
familiarity with AI technologies. (Radonjanin, Lazarevska , & Srbinoski, 2024). 

When considering AI judges specifically, North Macedonia's level of digital infrastructure maturity 
is  not  yet  fully  adequate.  AI  judge  systems  require  access  to  vast  digital  archives  containing 
millions  of  judicial  decisions  and  case  data  to  effectively  train  algorithms  (Ali,  Ristevski,  & 
Jolevski, 2022). Making court decisions publicly available in a digital and anonymized format is 
essential-not only for ensuring transparent use of data but also for training AI models. In this regard, 
a regional workshop held in Bosnia and Herzegovina in May 2025 reported that Western Balkan 
countries, including North Macedonia, are collectively working to improve their case management 
systems, publish anonymized court decisions online, and integrate AI and digital tools into judicial 
workflows. (Europe, 2025). 

The aforementioned workshop, organized under the Council of Europe’s CEPEJ program, aims to 
foster  regional  knowledge-sharing  on  digitalization  and  AI  integration.  It  is  evident  that  North 
Macedonia is actively working to build its capacity through peer learning within this framework. 
In light of these developments, for North Macedonia to establish an AI-supported judicial system, 
it must first complete its e-justice infrastructure, ensure the full functionality of electronic case and 
record  management  systems  across  all  courts,  and  elevate  its  data  protection  and  cybersecurity 
standards to the highest level. (Europe, 2025).  

Another critical factor is the ability of judges and court personnel to adapt to new technologies. 
Without adequate user training and cultural adaptation, even the most advanced AI systems will 
fail to deliver their intended benefits. Therefore, in conclusion, the applicability of AI judges in 
North Macedonia will largely depend on the success of current digitalization efforts and the extent 
of future investments in this domain. (National Convention on the EU in the RN Macedonia, Acc. 
2025). 

5.  Legal Framework 

To  date,  North  Macedonia  has  not  enacted  any  comprehensive  legal  framework  specifically 
addressing  artificial  intelligence.  As  of  May  2025,  there  is  no  law  or  binding  regulation  in  the 
country that directly governs the use of AI. The absence of dedicated legislation concerning the 
application  of  AI  in  the  legal  domain  highlights  the  urgent  need  to  establish  a  clear  regulatory 
framework. In this context, it is imperative that legal systems move swiftly and effectively toward 
adopting regulations capable of accommodating this emerging technology. (Turan, Kemaloğlu, & 
Küçüksille, 2020). At present, developments in the field of AI in North Macedonia are primarily 
guided by strategic documents and general regulatory instruments, such as laws on personal data 
protection. Notably, the preparation of a National AI Strategy, which began in 2021, has yet to 
result in a concrete legislative draft. Structural challenges-such as the lack of sufficient data and a 

shortage  of  qualified  experts-have  contributed  to  this  delay.  Nevertheless,  as  North  Macedonia 
continues  its  alignment  with  the  EU  acquis, it is  also  preparing  to  adopt the  European  Union’s 
approach to artificial intelligence regulation. (Law Gratis Reports., 2025). Experts emphasize that 
the legal gaps surrounding artificial intelligence in North Macedonia leave citizens vulnerable to 
potential  misuse,  and  that  an  AI  regulatory  framework  aligned  with  EU  standards  must  be 
developed urgently. In this regard, the country already has regulations in place that parallel the 
EU’s General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). North Macedonia’s Personal Data Protection 
Agency actively monitors the development and use of AI technologies to ensure a balance with 
individual privacy rights. Furthermore, the new Law on Personal Data Protection, which came into 
force  in  2020,  along  with  its  implementing  regulations,  sets  out  general  principles  concerning 
automated  data  processing  and  decision-making  procedures.  (Korbayram,  2024).  For  example, 
safeguards  aligned  with  the  GDPR  have  been  established  regarding  the  processing  of  sensitive 
personal data by AI and the issuance of fully automated decisions. However, highly specific issues-
such  as  delegating  judicial  decisions  to  AI-are  not  explicitly  addressed  within  the  current  legal 
framework. At present, the Law on Courts and related legislation in North Macedonia define the 
concept of a judge strictly as a human being and require that all judgments and rulings bear the 
signature of an authorized judge. This implies that, even if an AI system were to render a decision 
in practice, it would not be legally valid without the formal approval of a human judge. (Law Gratis 
Reports., 2025). 

At the European Union level, although a unified legal framework on artificial intelligence has not 
yet entered into force, the forthcoming EU Artificial Intelligence Act-expected to be finalized by 
2025-serves as a significant point of reference. Through this draft regulation, it is evident that the 
EU  has  adopted  a  more cautious  and  human  rights-oriented approach compared to  some  of the 
early-adopting countries in the field of AI. (Dönmez, 2025).  The draft AI Act classifies artificial 
intelligence systems according to their level of risk and explicitly designates the use of AI in the 
field of justice and the judiciary as a “high-risk” category. (EU AI Act, 2025). Accordingly, AI 
systems  used  by  judicial  authorities  for  decision-making  or  the  application  of  law-such  as 
algorithms that conduct legal precedent analysis, evaluate evidence, or render judgments in place 
of a judge-are considered high-risk and will be subject to strict regulatory requirements. (European 
Commission,  2024).  The  deployment  or  use  of  such  systems  will  be  required  to  meet  specific 
mandatory  criteria  regarding  transparency,  traceability,  human  oversight,  and  safety.  The  EU 
regulation  aims  to  prevent  the  uncontrolled  use  of  automated  decision-making  systems  in  both 
member and candidate countries. (EU AI Act, 2025). Therefore, if North Macedonia intends to 
pursue future projects involving AI judges or similar initiatives, it must take into account the high 
standards established in EU legislation. Additionally, the Council of Europe’s 2018 Ethical Charter 
serves as a valuable soft law instrument for member states, and North Macedonia is expected to 
develop its domestic regulations and ethical codes in alignment with these principles. 

When compared to practices in other countries, North Macedonia’s regulatory gaps become more 
apparent. For instance, neighboring Serbia published a National AI Ethics Guidelines document in 
2019,  outlining  principles  to  ensure  that  AI  systems  are  developed  in  a  fair,  accountable,  and 
transparent manner. (Radonjanin, Lazarevska , & Srbinoski, 2024). Similarly, some EU member 
states have taken legislative steps that indirectly regulate the use of AI in the judiciary. For example, 
in 2019, France enacted a provision in its Code of Justice (Article 33) that prohibits the use of AI 
to identify individual judges or statistically evaluate their performance based on their rulings. This 
regulation aims to protect judicial independence by preventing the scoring or ranking of judges 

through decision analytics. While countries like the United Kingdom and Germany have not yet 
introduced AI judges, they have launched pilot programs for online court platforms and automated 
mediation systems. 

Another important dimension of North Macedonia’s legal framework relates to regulatory gaps and 
institutional capacity. Currently, the country lacks a specific authority tasked with overseeing the 
use  of  artificial  intelligence  across  different  sectors  (Nuredin,  2023b),  including  the  judiciary. 
(Korbayram & Delev, 2023). While the Ministry of Information Society and Administration and 
the  Fund for  Innovation  and  Technology  Development  appear to  be responsible for  the  general 
coordination of AI policies, there is currently no independent regulatory or ethical body specifically 
designated  to  oversee  the  use  of  AI  in  the  judiciary.  This  institutional  gap  poses  challenges, 
particularly when it comes to establishing technical standards and addressing complaints that may 
arise  from  the  use  of  AI  in  court  proceedings.  Experts  emphasize  the  urgent  need  to  develop 
protective legislation to ensure that citizens are not subjected to rights violations stemming from 
the application of AI technologies. (Law Gratis Reports., 2025). This includes essential safeguards 
such as the right of citizens to appeal and seek review of automated decisions, the obligation to 
explain  algorithmic  outcomes,  and  the  certification  and  oversight  of  AI  systems.  As  North 
Macedonia moves toward full EU membership, it will need to strengthen its legal framework in 
these areas. 

In conclusion, given the absence of clear legal foundations for the use of AI in the judiciary, the 
implementation  of  AI  judges  currently  exists  in  a  legal  grey  area.  To  address  this  gap,  the 
completion of the National AI Strategy must be prioritized, followed by the adoption of dedicated 
legislation or amendments to existing judicial laws - defining the scope and limits of AI use in the 
justice system. This would provide legal clarity on key issues such as the types of cases suitable 
for AI involvement, the legal status of AI-generated outcomes, the allocation of responsibility, and 
the mechanisms for oversight and accountability. 

6. Discussion and Conclusions 

Within the framework of the findings presented in the previous sections of this study, it is evident 
that  the  integration  of  artificial  intelligence  (Nuredin,  2023a)  into  judicial  processes  offers 
significant  opportunities  in  terms  of  efficiency,  speed,  and  accessibility.  However,  this 
transformation also brings with it serious ethical concerns and legal uncertainties. 

In the context of North Macedonia, this assessment highlights three core dimensions in evaluating 
the applicability of AI-assisted judicial systems: ethical principles, technological infrastructure, and 
legal regulation. From an ethical standpoint, in order to establish public legitimacy, it is essential 
to build trust that AI systems are impartial and fair. In this regard, it is recommended that local 
ethical guidelines be developed in alignment with the Council of Europe’s key principles namely, 
respect  for  human  rights  (Nuredin,  2022a),  non-discrimination,  security,  transparency,  and  user 
control. 

Before being implemented in courts, AI systems should undergo bias testing, and both algorithmic 
transparency and decision explainability must be integral to the process. As a starting point, pilot 
programs  could  be  introduced  in  limited  areas  such  as  small  claims  disputes  through  a  judge-
assistant model, allowing gradual and controlled integration. 

From  a  technological  standpoint,  accelerating  digital  transformation  in  North  Macedonia  is 
essential. The digitization of court records, development of integrated case management systems, 
facilitation of inter-institutional data sharing, and investment in cybersecurity form the foundation 
of  this  process.  Citizen-oriented  platforms  such  as  an  e-Court  portal  would  not  only  enhance 
transparency but also help meet the data demands of AI systems. In this regard, EU funding and 
regional  cooperation  initiatives  can  provide  valuable  support.  In  terms  of  human  resources,  the 
recruitment of IT specialists in the public sector and the provision of continuous training should be 
prioritized. 

On the legal front, North Macedonia must first develop a National Artificial Intelligence Strategy, 
followed by the adoption of a dedicated AI Law or amendments to existing legislation. The legal 
status of AI-assisted decisions should be clearly defined, requiring final approval by a human judge 
and ensuring that individuals retain access to legal remedies. Alignment with the EU's AI Act and 
the adaptation of best practices from countries such as Estonia, France, and the Netherlands will be 
critical in guiding this transition. 

In conclusion, the implementation of AI judges in North Macedonia will require the strengthening 
of  both  technical  and  normative  infrastructures.  However,  if  this  transformation  is  managed 
properly with full respect for the right to a fair trial and the principle of judicial independence it 
can  lay  the  foundation  for  a  human-centered,  technology-assisted  justice  system.  The  proposed 
roadmap involves starting with limited pilot projects and continuously refining the system through 
ongoing monitoring and evaluation. In this way, AI can serve as the “assistant angel” to the human 
judge;  but  the  scales  of  justice  must  always  remain  under  the  guidance  of  human  intellect  and 
conscience.
